What the profit margin means for iPhone and iPad sales.The headline news was that while profits and revenue increased to $9.5 billion and $43.6 billion respectively over the same quarter last year, profit margins fell for the first time since 2008. Gross profit margins fell from 47.4% to 37.5% of revenue, compared with the same quarter last year while net profit margin also fell from 26.68 to 21.79%.

Gross profit margin is the ratio between sales and the cost of making the goods sold, while net profit margin includes all costs, including taxes. These margins are notable since Apple has by far the highest profit margins in the business. Samsung's profit margin, in contrast, was 17.4% in the fourth quarter of 2012.

Apple's margin drop seems to be mainly due to the mix of products the company sold in Q1, 2013 and the gross profit margin on each. Business Insider puts the decline down to the following trend:

A shift in product mix toward lower-margin iPads and lower-margin cheaper iPhones. Apple slashed the price of the iPhone 4 to try to sell more units in China. Also, thanks to the slowdown in sales of the iPhone, a greater percentage of Apple's revenue now.

Is The iPhone Pulling Its Weight? A set of charts published by Bare Figures show that the iPhone accounted for a massive 52.65% of Apple's revenue in Q2, 2013. Its next biggest product the iPad brings in a mere 20.06%. The iPhone became Apple's biggest product in terms of revenue in 2009 and the percentage of revenue for which it accounts has been climbing ever since. But there are a few problems with that picture.

Firstly, while Apple is selling more iPhones than ever (37.4 million in Q2, 2013), iPhone sales grew only 7% last quarter, well below the estimated 30% growth rate for the smartphone industry as a whole. In addition, consumers are buying a lot more older models than they did with previous releases. A study published by Consumer Intelligence Research Partners estimates that 53 percent of all iPhones sold worldwide are iPhone 5. The rest were iPhone 4 and iPhone 4S models.

This is a very different sales pattern than what we saw in the second quarter of the iPhone 4S’s availability. When that device was the latest model, it accounted for 73 percent of all iPhone sales in April 2012, a significantly larger share than the iPhone.”

The iPhone 5 brings has a much higher profit margin than older models so the fact that people are buying fewer of the latest iPhone model is hitting the company's overall profit margins. Last year GadgetMasters looked at the cost of making an iPhone 5.

The unlocked version of the iPhone 5 costs around $649 for 16GB, so the difference between manufacturing cost of $207 and $649 retail price is $442. That translates into a profit margin of around 68% per iPhone.

That margin is still astonishingly high in comparison to Apple's rivals. Bernstein Research analyst Mark Newman estimates that Samsung’s gross profit margin for the Galaxy S4 will be 46%.

Apple's other iPhone problem is that the biggest new smartphone markets will be in emerging countries like India and China but at the current pricing its devices are just too expensive for those markets. The company faces the unpalatable choice of slashing prices or losing market share. In China, Apple cut the price of its older iPhone 4 but revenues still only grew by just eight per cent on a year-over-year basis, according to the earnings release.

Tim Worstall at Forbes weighs in with the following analysis.

You can get a Chinese Android cheapo for $100 or less these days. Much of the world does not operate handset subsidies as is common in the US and Europe. You want a piece of hardware then you pay for it, all of it, on day one. Such a pricing system is obvious.[quote]

So fewer people in the U.S. and Europe are buying the latest versions of the iPhone and iPad and even older models may still be too expensive for the rest of the world unless Apple slashes prices, and therefore margins, considerably.

One way out of this bind is to introduce new products which will take over from the iPhone as Apple's cash cows. The company said in the earnings call that it would introduce entirely new categories of product starting this Fall.

What About Software Services?

Appe's management said during the earnings call that the company plans to new “product categories of hardware, software and services.” The services part may be crucial. One of the tricks Apple uses to boost gross profit margins is extra storage. Zdnet explains.

[quote]Once again, Apple -- like Google and Amazon -- is charging a premium price for extra storage. If you want to upgrade from the 16GB base model to the 32GB iPhone 5, this will cost you an extra $100, but the additional flash storage only costs Apple $10.

Media services drive a need for more storage. Darcy Travlos at Forbes notes:

Second, management, for the first time, brought “iTunes and software” to the forefront. They emphasized that the combined category accounted for more than $4B in sales in the quarter (slightly less than 10% of overall revenues), or a $16B annual run rate.

Another hint that new services may be a big part of Apple's new strategy was the emphasis which the company's management placed on metrics other than units and market share.

Throughout the call, they rattled off supporting statistics: #1 in sixth year in a row for customer satisfaction via JD Powers, 97% loyalty rate, and 82% of North American web traffic accessed via an iOS device.

ICloud’s user base also jumped 20% from 250 million last quarter to 300 million users. Over at ComputerWorld, Jonny Evans concludes the following:

It's hard to avoid the conclusion that Apple has been making significant investments in putting together the infrastructure it will require to deliver a series of unique services designed to differentiate its devices from the competition. The future of Apple depends on its successful evolution from being a device-centric firm into a services and solutions provider.

Are services the way forward for Apple? Are iPhone margins on a permanent downward slide? Will a new product replace the iPhone as Apple's breadwinner? We will be tracking this story as it develops.
